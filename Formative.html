The Great Green Wall

The Great Green Wall is an initiative that is based in Africa to combat climate change. The aim is to plant a wall of trees 8,000 km long and 15 km wide to combat desertification in Africa. When the Great Green Wall is finished it will be the largest living structure on the planet. Located in Sahel on the southern border of the Sahara desert the Great Green Wall has helped in many other ways on top of combatting climate change helping the lives of people living in the Sehal region. The Great Green Wall is an ambitous project which is aiming to have a massive impact in Africa and worldwide. The aim of this website is to educate you about the project, the benefits of the Great Green Wall, the history behind it and the future of the Great Green Wall.

Benefits
Increased stability The green wall will help stability by improving job opportunties with more green jobs available to people living in Africa. These jobs will help to give local people the opportunity and will to stay in their local area helping to break the cycle of migration.
Helping equality Gender equality will be improved by giving more opportunities to women. More access to water will mean women and girls don't have to spend hours gathering water. Through the economic opportunities created by the green wall poverty will be reduced helping create greater equality.
Fighting Climate Change Growing fertile land will help combat climate change and is an essential resource on the planet. It will also help by increasing sustainable energy in Africa reducing carbon dioxide emissions. Along with this we aim to grow sustainable consumption habits helping reduce overcombustion.

The History of the Great Green Wall

The reason for the creation of the Great Green Wall dates back to the 1970s when lots of the fertile land in the Sahel started to become severly degraded meaning there was a lot less fertile land. This was cause by a combination of factors. The land went dry and barren due to the combined effects of climate change, population growth and unsustainable land management practices. Barren land helped create a cycle of poverty due to a lack of land to grow food on, mass unemployment, conflict over natural resources, water shortages and forced migration. To combat this ideas that would be built on which would eventually turn into the Great Green Wall were in thought around the 1980s. In 2007 this idea was finally finialised under the leadership of the African Union and was signed by 11 African countries. Since then over 20 countries have signed up to the project and is developing everyday.

The Future

To help propell this project into the future the Great Green Wall multi-actor Accelerator (GGW Accelerator) was announced by the President of France Emmanuel Macron and other world leaders at the One Planet Summit on January 11th, 2021. This is an initiative to help accelerate the process of the Great Green Wall speed up. The main aim is to "help all actors for the Great Green Wall (GGW) Initiative to better coordinate, monitor and measure the impact of their actions." This will mean the project is much more likely to reach its intended outcome and with more and more trees planted every day. In turn this will help the day to day lives of people living in the Sehal region. The Accelerator has a five year plan from 2021 to 2025. The first step in 2021 is to design an GGW Online Platform for monitoring, tracking and connecting financing flows with project needs and implementation results. Then from 2021 to 2022 support GGW countries with monitoring and reporting systems for the GGW. At the same time from 2021 to 2023 check the implementation progress against the targets that have been set by the GGW. After this the Accelerator unit will be transferred to the Pan African Agency. Finally the last step planned for now is to review and evaluate the impact of the Accelerator investments.
